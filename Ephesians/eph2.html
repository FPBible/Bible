Ephesians

Chapter 2 — One New Humanity

Ephesians 2 describes a present covenant change — death to life, exclusion to inclusion, and full access to God established now in Christ.

From Death to Life

You were dead in your trespasses and sins, walking according to the age of this world, according to the ruler of the authority of the air — the spirit now at work in those characterised by disobedience. Among them we all once lived, carrying out the desires shaped by the flesh and the mind, and were by nature children marked out for wrath, like the rest. (Eph 2:1–3)

But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which He loved us, made us alive together with Christ even when we were dead in our trespasses — by grace you have been rescued. (Eph 2:4–5)

Raised and Seated — Now

He raised us up together with Him and seated us together with Him in the heavenly realm in Christ Jesus, so that in the unfolding New Covenant age He might display the surpassing riches of His grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus. (Eph 2:6–7)

For by grace you have been rescued through faith — and this is not from yourselves; it is God’s gift — not from works, so that no one may boast. For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand that we should walk in them. (Eph 2:8–10)

From Exclusion to Inclusion

Therefore remember that formerly you — the nations according to the flesh, called “uncircumcision” by those who call themselves “circumcision” — were at that time without Christ, excluded from the citizenship of Israel, strangers to the covenants of promise, having no hope and without God in the world. (Eph 2:11–12)

But now, in Christ Jesus, you who once were far away have been brought near by the blood of Christ. For He Himself is our peace, who made both groups one and broke down the dividing wall of hostility. (Eph 2:13–14)

The End of the Dividing Wall

By abolishing in His flesh the law of commandments expressed in ordinances, He created in Himself one new humanity in place of the two, making peace, and reconciled both to God in one body through the cross, thereby putting hostility to death. (Eph 2:15–16)

He came and proclaimed peace to those far away and peace to those near, for through Him we both have access in one Spirit to the Father. (Eph 2:17–18)

A Dwelling Place for God

So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but fellow citizens with the saints and members of God’s household, built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Christ Jesus Himself being the cornerstone, in whom the whole structure, being joined together, grows into a holy temple in the Lord. In Him you also are being built together into a dwelling place for God by the Spirit. (Eph 2:19–22)

* * *

Chapter Summary

  • Death and life describe covenant status, not biology. (Eph 2:1–5)
  • Believers are raised and seated with Christ as a present reality. (Eph 2:6)
  • Grace rescues and recreates a people for purposeful living. (Eph 2:8–10)
  • Gentiles are fully included without adopting old covenant markers. (Eph 2:11–14)
  • The law that divided has been abolished in Christ. (Eph 2:15–16)
  • God now dwells in a living people, not a physical temple. (Eph 2:19–22)

* * *

Fulfilled Commentary — Ephesians 2

1) “Dead” and “alive” are covenant realities

Paul is not describing biological corpses. Death here is separation from covenant life; life is restored relationship. This is why the change can happen “now” and “together with Christ.” (Eph 2:1–5)

2) Heavenly seating is shared authority, not a future location

To be seated with Christ means to share His rule and status. This is a statement of present identity, not a promise of post-mortem geography. (Eph 2:6)

3) Grace rescues into purpose, not passivity

Grace does not erase responsibility; it establishes it. The New Creation walks in works prepared, not to earn life, but because life has been given. (Eph 2:8–10)

4) The dividing wall was covenantal, not architectural

Paul’s wall is not masonry. It is the law that defined insider and outsider. By abolishing it, Christ did not erase holiness — He redefined it around Himself. (Eph 2:14–16)

5) The temple is now personal and corporate

God’s dwelling is no longer tied to a location. He inhabits a reconciled people. This completes the movement from tabernacle, to temple, to living community. (Eph 2:19–22)

Ephesians 2 announces the death of exclusion and the birth of one new humanity — not waiting to arrive, but already living from reconciliation.
